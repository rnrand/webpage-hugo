Multiplicative Inverses and Prime Numbers

This lecture develops a theory of how we can "divide by an integer $a$ modulo $m$". Of course, this language isn't defined so far, since we can't speak of "modding down" rational numbers. Instead we'll back up and reconsider what division should mean in this context.

Multiplicative Inverses Modulo $m$

Obviously we're not going to actually divide integers, since we don't want to create havoc with rational numbers. Instead, let's take a new perspective on what it means to divide by a number. Observe that if $a,b\in\mathbb{Z}$, then we can define "dividing by $a$" to be an operation that transforms $ab$ to $b$. Put another way, "dividing by $a$" can be phrased as "multiplying by $a^{-1}$". Under the usual interpretation, $a^{-1}$ is simply $1/a$, but we can't do that with equivalence classes. Instead, we're going to define a suitable stand-in that adapts the following point of view: $a^{-1}$ is a number such that $a\cdot a^{-1}=1$.

This discussion motivates the following definition.

Definition 9.1. Let $m,a$ be integers. We say that another integer $b$ is a multiplicative inverse of $a$ modulo $m$ if $ab \equiv_m 1$. When $a$ has a multiplicative inverse modulo $m$, we say that $a$ is invertible modulo $m$.

When the context and modulus are clear, we will sometimes just call $b$ "an inverse of $a$". We may go even further and write $a^{-1}$ for an inverse of $a$, with the understanding this implicitly depends on the modulus $m$.

So, for example, $3$ is an inverse of $2$ modulo $5$, since $2\cdot 3 \equiv_m 1$. Notice also that $-2$ and $8$ are also inverses of $2$ modulo $5$.

Example. Consider $m=4$. There are four equivalence classes: $0,1,2,3$. Observe that 1 and 3 have inverses modulo $4$: $1^{-1} \equiv_4 1$ (this is obvious) and $3^{-1} \equiv_4 3$, which we get by observing that $3 \cdot 3 \equiv_4 9 \equiv_4 1$. However, a little searching turns up no inverse for 2: \begin{align*} 2 \cdot 0 &\equiv_4 0 \\ 2 \cdot 1 &\equiv_4 2 \\ 2 \cdot 2 &\equiv_4 4 \equiv_4 0 \\ 2 \cdot 3 &\equiv_4 6 \equiv_4 2 \end{align*}

If you poke around though, you'll notice that similarly $6$ does not appear to have any inverses modulo $12$. There is pattern here, but it is non-obvious. To see it, we need the following definition.

Definition 9.2. Two integers $a$ and $b$ are relatively prime (or coprime) if $\gcd(a,b) = 1$.

Example. The numbers 10 and 21 are relatively prime, since $\operatorname{Div}(10) = \{\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 5, \pm 10\}$ and $\operatorname{Div}(21) = \{\pm 1, \pm 3, \pm 7, \pm 21\}$ and their only common divisors are $\pm 1$, of which $1$ is the largest.

Theorem 9.3. Let $m$ be a positive integer and $a$ be an integer. Then there exists a multiplicative inverse of $a$ modulo $m$ if and only if $a$ and $m$ are relatively prime.

Proof. Since this is an "if and only if" statement, we need to prove the implication in both directions. So let's first assume that $a$ and $m$ are relatively prime. By Bézout's Identity, there exist integers $s$ and $t$ such that $sa + tm = 1$. We claim that $s$ is a multiplicative inverse of $a$ modulo $m$. Indeed, since $sa\equiv_m 1$, $m$ divides $(sa-1)$, as $sa-1 = -tm$.

For the other direction, assume there exists multiplicative inverse of $a$ modulo $m$, say $b$. Then $m |(ab-1)$, so there exists $k\in\mathbb{Z}$ such that $km = ab-1$. This gives $ba+(-k)m=1$, showing that $$ 1 \in \{sa + tm \ \mid \ s,t\in\mathbb{Z}\}. $$ By our previous proof of Bézout's Identity, we know that $\gcd(a,m)$ is the smallest positive element of this set, meaning that it must be $1$. $$\tag*{$\Box$}$$

Cancellation and Uniqueness of Inverses

One application of inverses is to solving "equations" like $$6x \equiv_{17} 8.$$ These are usually called "congruences" since they are not asserting that anything is equal. In any case, intuitively we want to "divide" both sides by $6$ to solve for $x$. The next theorem justifies using inverses for this.

Theorem 9.3. Let $m$ be a positive integer. For all $a,b,c\in\mathbb{Z}$ such that $a$ is relatively prime to $m$, if $ab \equiv_m ac$, then $b \equiv_m c$.

This says that we can cancel $a$ from both sides. The analogous version of this from basic algebra says that if $a,b,c$ are real numbers such that $a$ is non-zero, then $ab=ac$ implies $b=c$. Note the connection between being non-zero and being invertible. In the case of real numbers, the conclusion may not be true of $a$ is zero. Similarly, when working modulo $m$ the conclusion may not be true if $a$ is not invertible. You should try out some examples, say with $m=12$ and $a=6,b=2,c=4$.

Proof. Since $a$ is relatively prime to $m$, it has an inverse modulo $m$, say $a^{-1}$. Then since $ab \equiv_m ac$, we have $a^{-1}ab \equiv_m a^{-1}ac$. But the left hand side is $a^{-1}ab \equiv_m 1\cdot b \equiv_m b$. Similarly, the right hand side is congruent to $c$ modulo $m$. Thus $b\equiv_m c$. $$\tag*{$\Box$}$$

Note that proofs like this implicitly use transitivity of $\equiv_m$, since they are chaining together relations.

To solve the congruence $6x \equiv_{17} 8$ from above, observe that $3$ is an inverse of $6$ modulo $17$. Thus we can transform this to an equivalent congruence $3\cdot 6x \equiv_{17} 3\cdot 8$, or $x \equiv_m 24$. Thus $x=24$ is a solution, and modding down further gives $x=7$ as another solution. After working a few of these, they are not too hard (see Rosen, 8th edition, Section 4.4.2 for some more examples). It is however important to understand how each of those steps is formally justified. (For example: Why is it the case that $3\cdot 6x \equiv_{17} 3\cdot 8$, i.e. why does multiplying by $3$ preserve equivalence?)

We can apply Theorem 9.3 to conclude that inverses are "unique up to congruence modulo $m$", as made precise in the following theorem.

Theorem 9.4. Let $m$ be a positive integer and $a$ be an integer relatively prime to $m$. If $b$ and $c$ are inverses of $a$ modulo $m$, then $b \equiv_m c$.

Proof. Since $ab\equiv_m 1$ and $ac \equiv_m 1$, we have $ab\equiv_m ac$ (by transitivity). By the previous theorem, this implies $b\equiv_m c$. $$\tag*{$\Box$}$$

Primes, Inverses Modulo a Prime, and Wilson's Theorem

The study of primes is a central topic of number theory. This section will prove what is arguably a first deeper result, called Wilson's Theorem, that builds on several facts about inverses modulo a prime.

Definition 9.5. An integer $p$ greater than 1 is called prime if the only positive divisors of $p$ are 1 and $p$. Otherwise, $p$ is called composite.

Example. Primes are obviously relatively prime to any positive number less than them, since they don't have any divisors except 1 and themselves. However, non-prime numbers (called composite numbers) can be relatively prime, even to each other. The numbers 10 and 21 are not prime, since $10 = 2 \cdot 5$ and $21 = 3 \cdot 7$. However, they are relatively prime, since $\operatorname{Div}(10) = \{\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 5, \pm 10\}$ and $\operatorname{Div}(21) = \{\pm 1, \pm 3, \pm 7, \pm 21\}$.

Now, let's prove something about primes. First, we need the following very useful theorem, a result from the ancient Euclid's Elements.

Theorem 9.6 (Euclid's lemma). If $p$ is prime, and $a,b$ are integers such that $p \mid a \cdot b$, then $p \mid a$ or $p \mid b$.

Proof. Without loss of generality, suppose $p \not \mid a$. Then $\gcd(p,a) = 1$ and there exist integers $x$ and $y$ such that $xa + yp = 1$. Then multiplying both sides by $b$, we have $$b = xab + ypb.$$ Since $p \mid ab$, let $n$ be an integer such that $ab = pn$. Then we have $$b = xab + ypb = xpn + ypb = p \cdot (xn+yb).$$ Therefore, by definition of divisibility, $p \mid b$. $$\tag*{$\Box$}$$

Notice that this proof makes use of Bézout's lemma. Since Bézout's lemma appears about 2000 years after Euclid, it is safe to say that this is not the proof that appears in the Elements.

One important property of primes is that we can divide by every integer modulo a prime, except for those congruent to zero.

Theorem 9.7. Let $p$ be a prime, and assume $p$ does not divide $a$. Then $a$ is invertible modulo $p$.

Proof. Since $p$ is prime, we have that $\gcd(p,a)\in\{1,p\}$. Since $p$ does not divide $a$, we have $\gcd(p,a)=1$ and hence $a$ is invertible modulo $p$. $$\tag*{$\Box$}$$

Example. The inverses modulo the prime $5$ of $1,2,3,4$ are $1,3,2,4$ respectively. Modulo the prime $7$, the inverses of $1,2,3,4,5,6$ are $1,4,5,2,3,6$ respectively.

In the above example, you may notice that an inverse of $4$ modulo $5$ is itself, i.e. $4$, and similarly $1$ is an inverse of itself. But also notice that for the other values, none is an inverse of itself. This is also true modulo $7$, and in fact for any prime, as the next two lemmas show.

Lemma 9.8. Let $m$ be an integer greater than $1$. Then $m-1$ is an inverse of itself modulo $m$.

Proof. Multiplying out $(m-1)(m-1)$ gives $(m-1)(m-1) = m^2 - 2m + 1$. Modulo $m$, this is congruent to $1$ since $m$ divides $m^2$ and $-2m$. $$\tag*{$\Box$}$$

An even quicker proof uses that $m-1 \equiv_m -1$, and that $(-1)^2=1$, so $(-1)(-1) \equiv_m 1$.

The next lemma is harder to prove, and the conclusion may not hold if $p$ is not prime. It shows that the numbers that are inverses of themselves modulo a prime must be congruent to $\pm 1$ modulo that prime.

Lemma 9.9. Let $p$ be a prime, and $a$ be integer such that $a \not\equiv_p 0$, and let $a^{-1}$ be an inverse of $a$ modulo $p$. Then if $a \not\equiv_p 1$ and $a \not\equiv_p p-1$, we have $a^{-1} \not\equiv_p a$.

Proof. Let's prove the contrapositive. If $a^{-1}\equiv_p a$, then $a^2 -1 \equiv_p 0$. Factoring gives $(a+1)(a-1) \equiv_p 0$. Thus $p|(a+1)(a-1)$, so by Euclid's Lemma $p|(a+1)$ or $p|(a-1)$. But this means $a+1\equiv_p 0$ or $a-1\equiv_p 0$, i.e. $a\equiv_p p-1$ or $a \equiv_p 1$. $$\tag*{$\Box$}$$

Our final result, the jewel of this lecture, is called Wilson's Theorem, because it was stated by John Wilson in the 1700s. It turns out that it was stated around 700 years prior by an Arab mathematician, Hasan Ibn al-Haytham. It uses the factorial function, which may be familiar to you from calculus.

Definition 9.10. We define the factorial function $n!$ recursively by

  • if $n = 0$, then $0! = 1$, and
  • if $n \gt 0$, then $n! = (n-1)! \cdot n$.

Theorem 9.11 (Wilson's Theorem). For all primes $p$, $(p-1)! \equiv_p -1 $.

Proof. This is clearly true if $p=2,3$, so assume $p\geq 5$. Each number in $\{2,\ldots, p-2\}$ has a multiplicative inverse that is not congruent to itself (Theorem 9.9). Moreover, since inverses are unique (Theorem 9.4), each number in $\{2,\ldots, p-2\}$ is an inverse of exactly one other number in that set. This implies that we can pair up the numbers with their inverses, creating $(p-3)/2$ disjoint pairs of elements that are inverses of each other.

Now consider multiplying the product $$ 2 \cdot 3 \cdots (p-2).$$ Since each pair in this product is congruent to $1$ modulo $p$, we have that the entire product is congruent to $1$ modulo $p$. Thus we get $$1 \cdot 2 \cdot \cdots \cdot (p-2) \cdot (p-1) \equiv_p 1 \cdot (p-1) \equiv_p 1 \cdot -1 \equiv_p -1.$$ $\tag*{$\Box$}$
