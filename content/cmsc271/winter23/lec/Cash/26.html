Matchings in Bipartite Graphs

This lecture introduces the notion of a matching in a graph. We then prove Hall's Marriage Theorem, which tells us when a bipartite graph has a matching that is as large as possible. Finally, time permitting, we sketch how one can find a matching in a bipartite graph efficiently.

Matchings

Consider a large-scale system that has some set of compute jobs to assign to a set of servers, and suppose that each job can only be run on some particular subset of servers, and each server can only run one job. We can model these constraints as a bipartite graph, where the jobs and servers form the two parts of the bipartition, and there is edge from a job to a server if that server is appropriate for the job.

An efficient system would like to assign as many jobs as possible to servers. This is equivalent to choosing a set of edges as large as possible such that each job and server have degree one or zero; The chosen edges indicate the job-server assignments that are taken.

This type of problem is extremely common in computer science, and in this lecture we study it abstractly. The next definition captures formally what we want to find.

Definition 26.1. A matching in a graph $G = (V,E)$ is a subset of edges $M \subseteq E$ such that no two edges in $E$ are incident at a vertex. A vertex that is incident to an edge in a matching $M$ is matched in $M$; otherwise, it is unmatched. For a subset $U\subseteq V$, we say that $U$ is matched in $M$ if every vertex in $U$ is matched in $M$.

A maximal matching is a matching that cannot be made larger; in other words, every edge in $E \setminus M$ is incident to a vertex matched in $M$. A maximum matching is a matching with the maximum number of edges.

A matching $M$ in a bipartite graph $G = (V,E)$ with bipartition $(A, B)$ is a complete matching if $A$ is matched in $M$.

The above definitions distinguish between maximal and maximum matchings. Just because you have a matching that can't be expanded doesn't mean that you necessarily have the largest possible matching!

However, this graph can exhibit a larger matching, which we have on the right. Since every vertex on the bottom is matched, this is a complete matching, and therefore, it is also a maximum matching. Hall's Marriage Theorem

The following result gives us a necessary and sufficient condition for a bipartite graph to have a complete matching and is due to Philip Hall, who was a British group theorist and worked at Bletchley Park during the Second World War.

Theorem 26.2 (Hall's Marriage Theorem). Let $G = (V,E)$ be a bipartite graph with bipartition $(A,B)$. Then $G$ contains a matching of $A$ if and only if $|N(S)| \geq |S|$ for all subsets $S \subseteq A$.

This theorem is our second example of a result showing that "the obvious necessary condition is also sufficient". In this case, the necessary condition is "$|N(S)| \geq |S|$ for all subsets $S \subseteq A$", which is also called the marriage condition. As we will see, it is not hard to show that this condition is necessary for a matching of $A$ to exist. But it turns out that, with some more work, you can show it is also sufficient, meaning that when the marriage condition holds, then a matching of $A$ must exist.

The proof that follows is a nice (and famous) example of strong induction, and exhibits some common techniques in graph theory proofs. It is also somewhat difficult compared to many of the proofs we've done.

In the proof, we use the notation $N_G(S)$ to mean the set of neighbors of $S$ in the graph $G$. We'll need this notation when go changing the original graph $G$ to produce new graphs $G_1,G_2$, and then consider how the set of neighbors $N_{G_1}(S)$, $N_{G_2}(S)$ relates to the original set $N_G(S)$.

Proof. First, consider the only if ($\Rightarrow$) direction. If the graph has no vertices then the theorem is trivial, so assume that the graph has at least one vertex. Suppose there exists a matching $M$ of $A$, and let $S\subseteq A$; we must show that $|N_G(S)| \geq |S|$. Since $M$ is a matching of $A$, we know that there is at least one edge (the one in $M$) for each vertex $v \in S$. This means there is at least one distinct neighbor in $B$ for each vertex $v \in A$. Therefore, $|N_G(S)| \geq |S|$.

The if ($\Leftarrow$) direction is proved by strong induction on $|A|$. The inductive statement (which depends on a positive integer $k$) is:

If $G$ is a bipartite graph with bipartition $(A,B)$, $|A|\lt k$, and $|N_G(S)| \geq |S|$ for all subsets $S \subseteq A$, then $G$ contains a matching of $A$.

Base case. We have $|A| = 1$. Taking $S=A$ in the marriage condition, we have $|N_G(A)| \geq |A|=1$, so there exists an edge $e$ from the lone vertex in $A$ to some vertex in $B$. Thus $M=\{e\}$ is a matching of $A$ in $G$, as desired.

Inductive step. Let $k \gt 1$, and assume the inductive statement is true for $1,\ldots,k-1$ (this is strong induction); We must prove the statement for $k$. Let $G$ be a graph with bipartition $(A,B)$ such that $|A| = k$, and assume $G$ satisfies the marriage condition. We must find a matching of $A$.

From here, we will consider two possible cases for $G$, and prove them separately. The cases are:

  • Case I: For all $S \subseteq A$ such that $S\neq \emptyset$ or $A$, we have $|N_G(S)|>|S|$
  • Case II: There exists $S \subseteq A$ such that $S\neq \emptyset$ or $A$ and $|N_G(S)|=|S|$

Intuitively, in the first case the marriage condition is "super-satisfied", since it says that $|N(S)|$ is strictly larger than $|S|$. The second case is where the marriage condition is just barely satisfied for some $S$. Note that we had to carefully exclude $S=\emptyset$ or $S=A$ to avoid trivialities.

Proof of Case I: By the condition in Case I, we know that for all $S\subseteq A$, $S\neq \emptyset$ or $A$, $|N_G(S)| \geq |S|+1$. In particular, we know that $G$ has at least one edge $e=uv$. Consider the graph $G'$ obtained by deleting $u,v$ along with all edges incident to them (including $e$). Then $G'$ is still bipartite, with bipartition $(A',B')=(A\setminus\{u\},B\setminus\{v\})$. We claim that $G'$ still satisfies the marriage condition. Indeed, if $S\subset A'$ is a non-empty, then $$ |N_{G'}(S)| \geq |N_{G}(S)| - 1, $$ since the set $S$ loses at most one neighbor ($v$) when we created $G'$. Since we assumed Case I, $G$ super-satisfies the marriage condition, and $|N_{G}(S)| \geq |S| + 1$. Thus we get $$ |N_{G'}(S)| \geq |S| +1- 1 = |S|. $$ We have shown that $G'$ satisfies the marriage condition. Moreover, it has $|A'| = k-1$, so we can apply the inductive assumption to $G'$ to obtain a matching $M'$ of $A'$ in $G'$. We finally take $M= M' \cup \{e\}$ to obtain a matching of $A$ in $G$, completing the inductive step for Case I.

Proof of Case II: By the condition in Case II, we can fix some $A'\subseteq A$, $A'\neq \emptyset$ or $A$ such that $|N_G(A')|=|A'|$. Let $B'=N_G(A')$. Define two graphs $G_1,G_2$ as follows:

  • $G_1$ is the graph with bipartition $(A',B')$ induced by these sets in $G$ (i.e. take all edges between $A'$ and $B'$).
  • $G_2$ is the graph with bipartition $(A\setminus A',B\setminus B')$ induced by these sets in $G$ (i.e. take all edges between $A\setminus A'$ and $B\setminus B'$).

Note that there are some edges of $G$ that may not appear in either $G_1$ or $G_2$: These are the edges from $A\setminus A'$ to $B'$. It turns out we won't need them.

Our plan is to find a matching $M_1$ of $A'$ in $G_1$, and a matching $M_2$ of $A \setminus A'$ in $G_2$. We can then take $M=M_1\cup M_2$ to get a matching of $A$ in $G$, as desired.

First consider $G_1$. Because $A'$ is a proper subset, $|A'| \lt k$. Moreover, $G_1$ satisfies the marriage condition easily, since $N_{G_1}(S)=N_G(S)$ for every $S\subseteq A'$. Thus, by strong induction, $G_1$ contains a matching $M_1$ of $A'$.

Now consider $G_2$. Once again, $|A\setminus A'| \lt k$. We now show that $G_2$ satisfies the marriage condition (this is trickier than in $G_1$ we because omitted some edges from $A\setminus A'$ when we formed $G_2$). Suppose for contradiction that there exists $S\subseteq (A\setminus A')$ such that $|N_{G_2}(S)| \lt |S|$. Observe that $$ N_G(S\cup A') = N_{G_2}(S) \cup N_G(A') $$ because every neighbor of $S \cup A'$ in $G$ is either adjacent to a vertex in $A'$ (and possibly also one in $S'$) and thus in $N_G(A')$, or adjacent to $S$ and not $A'$ and thus in $N_{G_2}(S)$.

We then have \begin{align*} |N_G(S\cup A')| & \leq |N_{G_2}(S)| + |N_G(A')| &\\ & = |N_{G_2}(S)| + |A'| & \text{(by choice of $A'$)} \\ & \lt |S| + |A'| & \text{(by choice of $S$)}\\ & = |S \cup A'| & \text{(because $S$ and $A'$ are disjoint)}. \end{align*} But this shows that $S\cup A'$ contradicts the marriage condition in $G$. This completes the proof of Case II.

The theorem is proved by taking $M=M_1\cup M_2$ to create a matching of $A$ in $G$. $\tag*{$\Box$}$

Finding Matchings in Bipartite Graphs

Obviously, not every bipartite graph will contain a complete matching. However, perhaps we would like to try to find a maximum matching. But if we just go and choose edges greedily (while avoiding having common endpoints, but otherwise employing no strategy), we may end up with a maximal matching that is not a maximum matching, meaning a matching that is not as large as possible but yet cannot be extended with more edges (to improve the matching one would need to backtrack).

How can we detect if we have reached a maximum matching and not just a maximal matching? The following result, proved by Claude Berge in 1957, gives us a way to do so.

Definition 26.3. Let $G$ be a graph with a matching $M$. An augmenting path for $M$ is a path on $v_0,v_1,\ldots, v_k$ such that $v_0$ and $v_k$ are unmatched in $M$ (i.e. $v_0, v_k$ do not belong to any matched edges) and the edges of the path alternate between edges in $M$ and edges not in $M$.

Theorem 26.4 (Berge's Lemma). Let $G = (V,E)$ be a graph with a matching $M$. Then $M$ is a maximum matching if and only if there is no $M$-augmenting path.

This result characterizes maximum matchings in terms of augmenting paths. An interesting feature of this result is that it doesn't depend on the whether $G$ is bipartite. In fact, if we look carefully at the definition of matchings, you'll notice that we never mentioned that the graph has to be bipartite!

The idea behind Berge's lemma is that if our graph and matching gives us an augmenting path, then we can take this path and flip the edges to get a larger matching.
