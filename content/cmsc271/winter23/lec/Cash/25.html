Connectivity and Bipartite Graphs

Connectivity, Paths, and Walks

A natural impulse when presented with a bunch of circles joined by lines is to see if we can get from one circle to another by following the lines. More formally, when we do this, what we're doing is checking if an object is connected to another object. It's not surprising to learn that this is a fundamental property of graphs that we'd like to work with.

Definition 25.1. A graph $G=(V,E)$ is connected if for every $u,v\in V$, $G$ contains a path from $u$ to $v$.

The graphs that we've seen so far have all been connected. However, we're not guaranteed to work with connected graphs, especially in real-world applications. In fact, we may want to test the connectedness of a graph. For that, we'll need to talk about the parts of a graph that are connected.

Definition 25.2. A connected component of a graph $G = (V,E)$ is a connected subgraph $G'$ of $G$ that is not contained in a larger connected subgraph of $G$.

Below it will be useful to consider a more permissive type of "path" in a graph that allows for repeats. Recall that we defined a path to be a graph, and by definition this doesn't allow repeated edges (let alone in a given order). The definition we need is given next.

Definition 25.4. A walk in a graph $G = (V,E)$ is a sequence of edges from $E$ of the form $v_0v_1,v_1v_2,\ldots,v_{\ell-1}v_\ell$. The length of a walk is $\ell$, the number of edges in the sequence (counting repetitions), and the end points of the walk are $v_0$ and $v_\ell$. A walk is closed if it starts and ends at the same vertex.

Technically, walks and paths are very different objects: While paths are graphs (i.e. pairs of the form $(V,E)$), walks are sequences of edges. But we will frequently mix them when convenient and confusion is unlikely. For instance, we will say that a graph "contains a walk from $v_0$ to $v_\ell$" if there exists a walk with those endpoints.

We first observe, without a detailed proof, that a walk without any repeated vertices immediately gives a path in an obvious way: Take the involved vertices along with the edges of the walk.

We will be interested in the relationship between walks and paths. Walks have some nice properties, such as the following.

Lemma 25.5. Let $G$ be a graph and let $s,m,t$ be vertices in $G$. If $G$ contains a walk from $s$ to $m$ of length $\ell_1$, and a walk from $m$ to $t$ of length $\ell_2$, then it contains a walk from $s$ to $t$ of length $\ell_1+\ell_2$.

The proof of this lemma is the obvious approach: Just concatenate the walks and check that you get a valid walk. We bother to state this lemma because it is not true for paths. The reason is that the paths might coincide at some vertices other than the endpoints, and taking their union (which is the closest we can do to concatenating them) may not result in a path at all (we might get cycles in the result, for instance).

Fortunately we can get the best of worlds (i.e. simplicity of combining walks along with the intuitively simple nature of paths) by doing a little technical work in the remainder of this section.

Lemma 25.6. Let $s$ and $t$ be distinct vertices in a graph $G$, and let $W$ be a walk in $G$ from $s$ to $t$. If $W$ has the shortest length amongst all walks from $s$ to $t$, then the edges of $W$ (along with the obvious vertices) form a path from $s$ to $t$.

Proof. By our observation above, we only need to show that $W$ does not repeat any vertices. Let $W=v_0v_1,v_1v_2,\ldots,v_{\ell-1}v_\ell$ be a walk from $v_0=s$ to $v_\ell=t$ of minimal length, and assume for contradiction that there is a repeated vertex, i.e. that there exist $0\leq i\lt j \leq\ell$ such that $v_i = v_j$. We use this to find a shorter walk from $s$ to $t$ and reach a contradiction. Consider the walk $$ W' =v_0v_1,\ \ldots,\ v_{i-1}v_i,\ v_jv_{j+1},\ \ldots,\ v_{\ell-1}v_\ell. $$ (If $i=0$ then this notation means we remove an initial segment of the walk up to $v_j$; If $j=\ell$ then we truncate a final segment of the walk after $v_i$.) This is a valid walk because $v_i=v_j$. It is also shorter than $W$ because it omits at least one edge. Moreover, it is still a walk from $s$ to $t$ (even when we remove the initial or final segment). This contradicts the assumed minimality of $W$. $\tag*{$\Box$}$

Corollary 25.7. A graph $G$ contains a path between distinct vertices $s,t$ if and only if it contains a walk from $s$ to $t$.

Proof. Given a path, we can form a walk in the obvious way. Given that a walk exists, there must exist a shortest walk, and the previous lemma guarantees that it gives a path. $\tag*{$\Box$}$

This next corollary makes precise that we can treat paths like walks, when it come to connecting them. (But note that the proof is not simply concatenating the paths!)

Corollary 25.8. If $G$ contains a path between $u$ and $v$, and also a contains a path from $v$ to $w$, then it contains a path from $u$ to $w$.

Proof. If the $u,v,w$ are not all distinct, then this is easy, so assume they are distinct. Then by the previous corollary $G$ contains walks from $u$ to $v$ and $v$ to $w$, so by Lemma 25.5 it contains a walk from $u$ to $w$. By the previous corollary again, $G$ contains a path from $u$ to $w$. $\tag*{$\Box$}$

In case that seemed fussy, it's important to realize here (and elsewhere in graph theory) that intuition is useful but sometimes leads to over-claiming. Corollary 25.8 should be true, but if you think about it, it is more complicated that it might seem (in contrast to walks, where Lemma 25.5 was indeed obvious and easy). What Corollary 25.8 is really saying is that, given paths that may be very complicated and intertwined and meet at a common end, we can also sort them out and find a direct path between their other endpoints.

Bipartite Graphs

A common class of graphs involve two different types of objects that may be related to each other. For example, consider the graph with vertices consisting of students and universities, and edges representing if a student was admitted to that university. Visually, this type of graph is relatively simple: You can imagine the students on the left, the schools on the right, and the edges crossing the middle.

These types of graphs are called bipartite and form an important and useful class of graphs.

Definition 25.9. A graph $G = (V,E)$ is bipartite if there exist disjoint sets $A,B$ such that $V=A\cup B$ and there are no edges within $A$ or $B$ (more formally, this means for every $uv\in E$, it is never the case that $u,v\in A$ or $u,v\in B$.) We say that $(A, B)$ is a bipartition of $V$.

Whether or not a graph is bipartite is isomorphism invariant (exercise). Similar to the idea of complete graphs, we can define complete bipartite graphs.

Definition 25.10. The complete bipartite graph on $m$ and $n$ vertices is the graph $K_{m,n} = (V,E)$, where $(V_m,V_n)$ is a bipartition of $V$ with $|V_m| = m$ and $|V_n| = n$, and $$E = \{(u,v) \mid u \in V_m, v \in V_n \}.$$

Bipartite graphs come up naturally in a lot of applications because a bipartite graph models relationships between two groups of things: job applicants and potential employers, PhD students and PhD advisors, compute jobs to be scheduled and available servers, etc. In many of these applications, we would like to assign or match an object from the first set with an object from the second. We will return to this type of "matching" problem in the next lecture.

A natural way to think of a bipartition is as a coloring of the vertices of a graph. Given a bipartition $(A,B)$, let's say that the vertices in $A$ are colored red, and those in $B$ are colored blue. The bipartite condition is that no red vertices are adjacent, and no blue vertices are adjacent.

With this view, we can get intuition for our main theorem of the lecture, which is as follows.

Theorem 25.11. A graph $G$ is bipartite if and only if $G$ does not contain a cycle of odd length.

This theorem is interesting because it gives us a characterization of being bipartite that is arguably simpler.

One direction of this theorem is easier to prove: If $G$ contains an odd cycle then it can't be bipartite. To see why intuitively, fix some odd cycle and a potential bipartition. Using coloring point of view, suppose we start at a vertex on the cycle that is red, and we proceed around the cycle. The vertices must alternate colors if this is a valid bipartition. But since the cycle has odd length, it will imply that the first vertex must also be blue, a contradiction.

The reverse direction (if there is no odd cycle then $G$ is bipartite) takes a little more creativity. And because the first direction is relatively easy, this type of theorem is sometimes said to show that "the obvious necessary condition (i.e. no odd cycles) is also sufficient (i.e. implies bipartite)". Many famous graph theory theorems have this form, and we'll see another in the next lecture.

We proceed with the proof of Theorem 25.11.

Proof of Theorem 25.11. First, we show the "only if" direction, following the sketch above. Assume $v_0v_1, \ \ldots,\ v_{\ell-1} v_0$ form a cycle in $G$ of odd length, and consider a bipartition $(A,B)$. WLOG, assume that $v_0\in A$. We claim that, for $j=0,\ldots,\ell-1$, if $j$ is even then $v_j\in A$, and if $j$ is odd then $v_j\in B$. You can prove this by induction (exercise). Since the cycle has odd length, $\ell-1$ is even and $v_{\ell-1}\in A$. But we also have $v_0\in A$ and an edge between $v_{\ell-1}$ and $v_0$, which contradicts the choice of $(A,B)$ as a valid bipartition.

For the "if" direction, we need to show that if $G$ has no odd cycles then we can find a valid bipartition of its vertices. This will take more work, and we start with two technical claims. We then finish the proof of Theorem 25.11 and return to the claims afterwards.

Claim 1. If $G$ contains a closed walk of odd length, then it contains a cycle of odd length.

Claim 2. If $G$ contains an odd-length path from $u$ to $v$ and also an even-length path from $u$ to $v$, then $G$ contains an odd-length cycle.

We can now give the bipartition. Assume WLOG that $G$ is connected, since $G$ is bipartite if and only if all of its connected components are bipartite (exercise). Thus $G$ contains a path between every pair of vertices. If $G$ is empty (no vertices) then it is clearly bipartite, so assume it has some vertex $v_0$ (chosen arbitrarily). We define the bipartition by $$ A = \{u\in V\ :\ \text{$\exists$ an even-length path from $v_0$ to $u$}\} $$ and $$ B = \{u\in V\ :\ \text{$\exists$ an odd-length path from $v_0$ to $u$}\} $$ We have that $A\cup B = V$ because we assumed $G$ is connected. We have that $A$ and $B$ are disjoint by Claim 2.

It remains to show that there are no edges within $A$ and $B$. Consider $A$ first ($B$ is similar). If there is an edge between two vertices $u,v$ in $A$, then this means there is an even-length path $P_1$ from $v_0$ to $u$, and an even-length path $P_2$ from $v_0$ to $v$. From these we can form an odd-length closed walk from that begins and ends at $v_0$: Take the edges of $P_1$, followed by $uv$, followed by the edges of $P_1$. This closed walk has odd length (since $P_1$ and $P_2$ have even length and we added one edge). By Claim 1, this walk contains an odd cycle, contradicting our assumption. Therefore there are no edges within $A$ (or $B$), and our bipartition is valid. This completes the proof. $\tag*{$\Box$}$

We now prove the two claims.

Claim 1. If $G$ contains a closed walk of odd length, then it contains a cycle of odd length.

Proof. Suppose $G$ contains a closed walk of odd length. Then it contains such a walk of minimum length, say $W= v_0v_1,\ \ldots,\ v_{\ell-1}v_0$. We claim that no vertex (other than $v_0$ at the ends) is repeated in this walk, and thus these edges (along with the obvious vertices) form a cycle of odd length.

Suppose for contradiction that for some $0 \leq i \lt j \leq \ell-1$, $v_i = v_j$. Consider the walks $$ W_1 =v_0v_1,\ \ldots,\ v_{i-1}v_i, \ v_j v_{j+1},\ \ldots, \ v_{\ell-1} v_0$$ and $$ W_2 = v_{i}v_{i+1}, \ \ldots, \ v_{j-1} v_{j}. $$ (If $i=0$, these walks are $W_1 = v_j v_{j+1},\ldots v_{\ell-1}v_0$ and $W_2 =v_0v_1,\ \ldots,\ v_{j-1}v_j$.) Both of these walks are valid, closed, and shorter than $W$. Moreover, their lengths sum to the length of $W$, which is odd, so one of them must be odd length. This contradicts our choice of $W$ as the shortest odd-length closed walk, and completes the proof. $\tag*{$\Box$}$

Claim 2. If $G$ contains an odd-length path from $u$ to $v$ and also an even-length path from $u$ to $v$, then $G$ contains an odd length cycle.

Proof. This is a corollary of Claim 1. Form a closed walk from $u$ to itself by taking the edges of the paths, one after the other. This is closed a walk of odd length, so by Claim 1, $G$ must contain a cycle of odd length. $\tag*{$\Box$}$
