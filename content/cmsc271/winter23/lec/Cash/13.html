Introduction to Counting and Probability

In this lecture we turn to the next major section of CMSC 27100: Combinatorics and Probability. Probability has become an essential part of a computer scientist's toolbox. Whether studying data structures, cryptography, machine learning, or any number of other subareas of CS, a basic understanding of probability is required for deep understanding.

Combinatorics is perhaps a less familiar word. For the purpose of this class, it is a fancy word for "counting." That is, it consists of solving problems that, in various forms, describe a set to you and ask you how many elements it contains. This sounds simple but there are quite a few interesting methods to learn.

We start with a description of "Naive Probability", which gives the first hint of why we study probability and combinatorics together.

I highly recommended that you read the textbook Introduction to Probability by Blitzstein and Hwang in tandem with this lecture, which covers sections 1.1 through 1.4.1. The text is freely available online.

Naive Probability

Probability models all sorts of phenomena related to unpredictability and uncertainty. We are going to start with a simple class of random experiments: Those where every outcome is equally likely. We'll call this Naive Probability and later come back to think about situations where events aren't equally likely.

To model a random experiment, one first defines a set of possible outcomes.

Definition. A sample space for a random experiment is a set $\Omega$, the members of which correspond to the possible outcomes.

Example.

  • Consider flipping a fair coin, which has two equally weighted outcomes. We could take $\Omega_1 = \{H,T\}$, or perhaps $\Omega_1 = \{0,1\}$, depending on our taste.
  • If we roll a fair six-sided die, a good sample space is $\Omega_2 = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$.
  • Now consider flipping two coins. A correct sample space for this experiment is $\Omega_3 = \{HH,HT,TH,TT\}$. Note that we need two outcomes $HT$ and $TH$; These are different, and they must be for proper modeling, as we will discuss later.
  • Similarly, consider rolling two fair six-sided dice. The sample space in this case is $\Omega_4 = \{(1,1), (1,2), \ldots, (6,5), (6,6)\}$.
  • If we pick a card at random from a standard deck of cards, a sample space could be $$ \Omega_5 = \{A\spadesuit,A\heartsuit,\ldots, 2\clubsuit\}, $$ or any other set that intuitively corresponds to the 52 cards in the deck.

Once we have a sample space, we could stop there and just consider individual outcomes. However, much of the richness of probability comes from using sets of outcomes, which correspond to English-language descriptions of features of outcomes. This motivates the following definition.

Definition. Let $\Omega$ be the sample space for a random experiment. An event is a subset of $\Omega$.

Example. These examples use $\Omega_1,\ldots,\Omega_4$ from the previous examples.

  • In $\Omega_1$, the event "the flip showed Heads" is simply $\{H\}$. In general, we can always use singleton sets to define events in this way.
  • In $\Omega_2$, the event "the roll was even" is $\{2,4,6\}$.
  • In $\Omega_3$, the event "the flips were the same " is $\{HH,TT\}$.
  • In $\Omega_4$, the event "the rolls sum to 4" is $\{(1,3), (2,2), (3,1)\}$.

Events can be combined via unions, intersections, and complements to give new events. These operations have a very intuitive interpretation: If $E_1,E_2 \subseteq \Omega$ are events on the same sample space, then $E_1 \cup E_2$ represents "$E_1$ or $E_2$", $E_1 \cap E_2$ represents "$E_1$ and $E_2$", and $E_1^c$ represents "not $E_1$". (The complement is taken with respect to the universe $\Omega$.)

Example. (This example is from [BH].) Suppose we flip $10$ coins. The sample space will be all length-$10$ strings consisting of $H$ and $T$ letters. That is, $\Omega$ contains strings like $HTTHTTTHHT$.

  • For each $i=1,\ldots,10$ we can define $A_i$ to be the event that the $i$-th toss shows Heads. Then, say, $A_1$ consists of strings in $\Omega$ that start with $H$.
  • Let $B$ be the event that at least one of the tosses shows Heads. Then $$ B = A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots \cup A_{10} = \bigcup_{i=1}^{10} A_i. $$ (The notation $\bigcup_{i=1}^{10} A_i$ works like the summation notation; It runs the counter form $1$ to $10$ and unions up all the corresponding sets.)
  • Let $C$ be the event that all of the tosses show Heads. Then $$ C = A_1 \cap A_2 \cap \cdots \cap A_{10} = \bigcap_{i=1}^{10} A_i. $$ In fact, $C$ is just $\{HHHHHHHHHH\}$.
  • Let $D$ be the event that at least two consecutive tosses show Heads. Then $$ C = (A_1 \cap A_2) \cup (A_2 \cap A_3) \cup \cdots \cup (A_9 \cap A_{10}) = \bigcup_{i=1}^9 A_i\cap A_{i+1}. $$
Finally, we define the probability of an event.

Definition. Let $\Omega$ be the sample space for a random experiment and $E\subseteq \Omega$ be an event. The (naive) probability of $E$ is defined to be $$ \mathrm{Pr}(E) = \frac{|E|}{|\Omega|}. $$

Of course, for this to make sense, $\Omega$ must be finite. We will return to the infinite case later. You can work through some of the small examples above to calculate probabilities by hand, but to do the larger ones you'll need to infer the sizes of the events and samples spaces indirectly. This explains why counting is the main technical challenge in naive probability.

How to Count: The Multiplication Rule

Suppose you are ordering an ice cream cone. There are three flavors of ice cream (chocolate, vanilla, strawberry), and two types of cones (cake and waffle). How many different orders are possible? Intuitively, it's $3\cdot 2 = 6$.

To see this in an organized way, you can draw a tree, like in Figure 1.4 on page 10 of [BH].

Theorem 12.1 (Multiplication Rule). If $A_1, A_2, \dots, A_k$ are finite sets, then $$|A_1 \times A_2 \times \cdots \times A_k| = |A_1| \cdot |A_2| \cdots |A_k|.$$

Returning to our examples above, we get that $\Omega_4 = \Omega_2\times\Omega_2$, so $|\Omega_4|=6\cdot 6 = 36$.

The Multiplication Rule gets applied in ad hoc ways to all sorts of problems where one is making a sequence of decisions (like our ice cream cones). There are two patterns that are so common in probability and combinatorics that they are given names: Sampling with and without replacement.

Theorem 12.2 (Sampling With Replacement). If we make $k$ choices, one after the other, from a set of $n$ objects, and we allow the same object to be chosen multiple times, then there are $n^k$ possible outcomes.

This theorem is just the Multiplication Rule with all of the sets $A_1,\ldots,A_k$ taken as the same set of objects.

Theorem 12.3 (Sampling Without Replacement). If we make $k$ choices, one after the other, from a set of $n$ objects, and we do not allow the same object to be chosen multiple times, then there are $n\cdot(n-1)\cdots (n-k+1)$ possible outcomes.

Another way to write $n\cdot(n-1)\cdots (n-k+1)$ is $$ \frac{n!}{(n-k)!}. $$ Later this notation will be more convenient, and it's also easier to remember since you don't have to mess with the $n-k+1$ factor each time.

Justifying the formula Theorem 12.3 is a little harder than Theorem 12.2. If you think about, the set are modeling is no longer a Cartesian product of sets. But it is not too hard to justify intuitively; You can think of making the first selection ($n$ choices), and then the second selection ($n-1$ choices), and then drawing a tree as we did with ice cream cones.

Example.

  • How many $6$ character passwords can we make using only lowercase letters? This is sampling with replacement, with $n=26$ and $k=6$; so there are $26^6$.
  • How many $6$ character passwords can we make using only lowercase letters, if we don't ever repeat a letter? This is sampling without replacement, again with $n=26$ and $k=6$; so there are $26\cdot 25\cdot 24\cdot 23\cdot 22\cdot 21$ possibilities.

Example. Suppose that I have a set $A = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_k\}$ with $k$ objects in it. How many subsets of $A$ are there? Let's consider the following approach. For each subset $S \subseteq A$, let $w_S$ be a binary (i.e. zero/one) string of $k$ bits $a_1 a_2 \cdots a_k$, where for $1 \leq i \leq k$, \begin{equation*} a_i = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if $x_i \not \in S$,} \\ 1 &\text{if $x_i \in S$.} \end{cases} \end{equation*} For instance, $w_\emptyset = 00 \cdots 0 = 0^k$, while $w_A = 11\cdots 1 = 1^k$. How many strings of the form $w_S$ are there? This is sampling with replacement where $n=2$ and $k$ is given, so there are exactly $2^k$ strings of length $k$. The string $w_S$ is sometimes called the characteristic string of $S$.

Occasionally counting problems will ask you to find the size of a set $A$ which can be written as the disjoint union of other sets. The next rule formalizes what is very intuitive: You can just count the sets in the union, and then add up their sizes.

Theorem 12.4 (Addition Rule). If $A_1, A_2, \dots, A_k$ are disjoint finite sets, then $$|A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots \cup A_k| = |A_1| + |A_2| + |A_k|.$$

I consider this self-evident, so I won't offer a proof.

Example.

  • If passwords must consist of either 6 lowercase letters or 6 digits (both with repetitions allowed), then there are $26^6+10^6$ possible passwords.

The Complement Trick and The Birthday Problem

Consider the following question: How many $6$ character passwords can we form using only lowercase letters and the digits $0$ through $9$? This is no harder than before; It is $(26+10)^6 = 36^6$.

What if we add the condition that the password contains at least one digit? Attacking this question directly using sampling with or without replacement doesn't work. If we try that approach, then the first option has $36$ choices. But how many options does the second choice have? Well, it's still $36$. But if you can continue until the end sixth choice, when your number of choices will depend on the past: If you picked a digit in one or more of the first five spots, then you have $36$ options. If not, then you must pick a digit, and you only have $10$ options.

The result is a tree that is not regularly shaped: At the last level, some nodes will have $36$ branches and some will have only $10$. This means we can't apply the Multiplication Rule, at least not directly.

There is a trick to use when you notice this happening in your counting:

Theorem 12.5 (Complement Rule). If $U, A$ are finite sets, and $A \subseteq U$, then $$|U \setminus A| = |U| - |A|.$$ In the language of complements, this is $$|A^c| = |U|-|A|$$ or, equivalently, $$|A| = |U|-|A^c|,$$ where $U$ is the universe under consideration.

I also consider this self-evident, but one can give a proof from the Addition Rule.

Example.

  • Let us count the number of $6$ character passwords consisting of lowercase letters and digits, that have at least one digit. Let's do this by counting the compliment: How many passwords are there that do not contain a digit? That's easy, those are just passwords with all letters, and there are $26^6$ of them. The set of all passwords consisting of letters and digits has size $36^6$, as calculated above. Putting this together gives $36^6-26^6$.

Now we'll apply this method to a famous calculation called the "Birthday Problem" or "Birthday Paradox." It starts with following question:

If there are $k$ people in a room, what is the probability that at least two people have the same birthday?
A more specific question is:
What is the minimal value of $k$ such that there is at least a $1/2$ probability that two people have the same birthday?

Once we have $366$ people the probability is $1$. Also, with only one person the probability is zero. The problem is to understand how the probability changes with $k$ between those two extremes. Let's remove some real-world complications by assuming that there are no leap days and that everyone's birthday is equally likely to be any day of the year.

To solve this, we first need a sample space. We'll take $\Omega = \{1,\ldots,365\}^k$, since we are sampling $k$ birthdays with replacement, and we have $|\Omega| = 365^k$.

Now define $E\subseteq \Omega$ to be the event that at least two people share a birthday. Formally, $E$ is the set of $k$-tuples with entries from $\{1,\ldots,365\}$ which repeat a value at some point. Following our thinking above, we will calculate $|E^c|$ and use the Complement Rule.

What is $E^c$? It is the set of tuples from $\Omega$ that do not contain any repeats. This is sampling without replacement, so $|E^c| = 365\cdot 364\cdots \cdot (365-k+1)$.

Now we get \begin{align*} \mathrm{Pr}(E) & = \frac{|E|}{|\Omega|} \\ & = \frac{|\Omega|-|E^c|}{|\Omega|} \\ & = 1 - \frac{|E^c|}{|\Omega|} \\ & = 1 - \frac{365\cdot 364\cdots \cdot (365-k+1)}{365^k}. \end{align*}

This graph is intuitively surprising to most people: Once $k=23$, the probability goes over $1/2$! Those $23$ people only cover a small fraction of the entire space, and yet collisions are very likely. Also note that this graph tops out at $100$; By the point the probability is essentially one.

Of course, predicting birthday collisions isn't that important in practice. But this phenomenon arises in several settings where coincidences are much more likely that one expects. One instance I have personal experience with is the class of Birthday Attacks in cryptography, but that is a subject for another course.

At this point it is fair to object to this being solution, since it does not really explain why such a small $k$ works. When we return to studying general probability we will be able to give a better answer (i.e. a solution that does not use counting in the same way). For now, you might be able to justify to yourself that the probability should (sort of) grow proportionally to $k^2$, not $k$, because amongst $k$ people there are (about) $k^2$ chances at a collision. With that intuition, it it less surprising that $k=23$ works, since $23^2 = 529$. But that reasoning is extremely sketchy!
