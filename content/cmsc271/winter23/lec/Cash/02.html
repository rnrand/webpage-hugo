In this lecture we'll first wrap up our coverage of connectives, and then discuss how the truth values of propositions are related. This leads us to the notion of a proof, which is an argument establishing the truth of a proposition based on the truth of other propositions.

The Conditional and Biconditional Connectives

Last time we defined the conditional connective $\rightarrow$; Next we consider a simpler related connective:

Definition 1.6. $p \leftrightarrow q$ is called a biconditional or logical equivalence and is pronounced "$p$ if and only if $q$". It is defined to be true exactly when $p$ and $q$ have the same truth values (i.e. both false or both true).

The truth table for this one is

$p$ $q$ $\quad$ $p \leftrightarrow q$
$T$ $T$ $T$
$T$ $F$ $F$
$F$ $T$ $F$
$F$ $F$ $T$

Example. Again let $p =$ "It is Wednesday." and $q =$ "I will be a milkshake." Then $p\rightarrow q$ is "If it is Wednesday, then I will buy a milkshake", and $p\leftrightarrow q$ is "It is Wednesday, if and only if I will buy a milkshake".

Now examine $p \rightarrow q$ in the case that $p$ is false - Say it's Monday, not Wednesday. In that case, we declare $p \rightarrow q$ to be true, regardless of whether or not we buy a milkshake. This follows the conventions discussed above.

The biconditional $p\leftrightarrow q$ can be reworded as "I always buy a milkshake on Wednesdays, and if it is Wednesday, then I must be buying a milkshake." which is closer to normal English. Intuitively it says that buying a milkshake and the day being Wednesday are equivalent in some sense. ∎

We close this section with a few more definitions:

Definition 1.7. Let $p$ and $q$ be propositions. We define

  • The converse of $p\rightarrow q$ is $q\rightarrow p$.
  • The inverse of $p\rightarrow q$ is $\neg p\rightarrow \neg q$.
  • The contrapositive of $p\rightarrow q$ is $\neg q\rightarrow \neg p$.

Example. Again let $p =$ "It is Wednesday." and $q =$ "I will be a milkshake."

  • The converse of $p\rightarrow q$ is "If I buy a milkshake, then it is Wednesday", or more naturally, "If I buy a milkshake, then it must be Wednesday".
  • The inverse of $p \rightarrow q$ is "If it's not Wednesday, then I won't buy a milkshake."
  • The contrapositive of $p \rightarrow q$ is "If I don't buy a milkshake, then it is not Wednesday", or "If I don't buy a milkshake, then it can't be Wednesday".
Next time we will discuss logical equivalence, but its worth taking a second to see if you have some intuition about those statements relate to other.∎

More Complex Propositions and Order of Operations

We can string together propositions using the connectives $\neg, \wedge,\vee,\rightarrow,\leftrightarrow$ to model more complex assertions. As with arithmetic, we need to be careful about the order of operations.

An example of a more complex proposition we'd like to model is "If it is either Wednesday and it is not raining, then I buy a milkshake." This could be modeled as $(p \vee (\neg q)) \rightarrow r$, where $p =$ "It is Wednesday." and $q =$ "It is raining." and $r =$ "I will be a milkshake."

In order to avoid being overwhelmed by parentheses, we introduce an order of precedence amongst the connectives: From highest to lowest, we take them in the order $\neg, \wedge,\vee$ and let $\rightarrow,\leftrightarrow$ have equal precedence (and thus ties are broken by reading them left-to-right). So, for example, $\neg p \wedge q$ means $(\neg p) \wedge q$ and not $\neg (p \wedge q)$ because $\neg$ has higher precedence than $\wedge$. Something like $p\rightarrow q \leftrightarrow r$ will be avoided, because we have to assign precedence from left to right, meaning it is $(p\rightarrow q) \leftrightarrow r$ and not $p\rightarrow (q \leftrightarrow r)$. In these cases we'll try to use parentheses always.

Arbitrarily complex propositions are allowed; For instance we could consider $((p\wedge q)\rightarrow r) \leftrightarrow (r \rightarrow q)$ or other even larger combinations of connectives.

To compute the truth table of a compound proposition, we proceed by computing the truth tables of its components and iteratively put them together, as in the following example.

Example. Let's compute the truth table of $\neg p\wedge q$. We start by computing the truth table of $\neg p$ as before:

$p$ $q$ $\quad$ $\neg p$
$T$ $T$ $F$
$T$ $F$ $F$
$F$ $T$ $T$
$F$ $F$ $T$
Now we use this column, along with the existing column for $p$, the compute the truth table we are after. We simply extend the table with another column:
$p$ $q$ $\quad$ $\neg p$ $\quad$ $\neg p\wedge q$
$T$ $T$ $F$ $F$
$T$ $F$ $F$ $F$
$F$ $T$ $T$ $T$
$F$ $F$ $T$ $F$

Once you do a few of these, they become a simple matter of robotically composing together truth tables until you have the one you're after. Here's a more complicated example, given in one table, for the proposition $((p \wedge q) \rightarrow r) \leftrightarrow (p \rightarrow (q \rightarrow r))$.

$p$$q$$r$$p \wedge q$$(p \wedge q) \to r$$q \to r$$p \to (q \to r)$$(p \wedge q) \to r \leftrightarrow p \to (q \to r)$
$T$$T$$T$$T$ $T$ $T$ $T$ $T$
$T$$T$$F$$T$ $F$ $F$ $F$ $T$
$T$$F$$T$$F$ $T$ $T$ $T$ $T$
$T$$F$$F$$F$ $T$ $T$ $T$ $T$
$F$$T$$T$$F$ $T$ $T$ $T$ $T$
$F$$T$$F$$F$ $T$ $F$ $T$ $T$
$F$$F$$T$$F$ $T$ $T$ $T$ $T$
$F$$F$$F$$F$ $T$ $T$ $T$ $T$
Note that since we had three atomic propositions $p,q,r$ we needed eight rows in our table this time. Also note that, curiously, this proposition is always true. ∎

Translating from English

Recall that the point of studying propositional logic at this stage is so that we can later understand what mathematical statements are saying precisely. The application of logic will thus involve translating between English and the language of propositional logic (at least in your head). A well-written statement should make this easy, but it is good to have practice a bit first, and there are some gotchas in common mathematical language.

Example. Consider the proposition:

Hina knows Python if she is a CS major and took 141, and she is a CS major or did not take 141.
A mathematical statement of this form would be rejected outright as too complicated; If you find yourself twisting around words like this, it's a sign that you should refactor your claims into something plainer. But let's try to parse it, just for the challenge. The atomic propositions are
  • $p=$Hina knows Python.
  • $q=$Hina is a CS major.
  • $r=$Hina took 141.
Next, if you look at the statement, it breaks apart into two independent pieces at the comma, joined by a conjunction. The first piece (Hina knows Python if she is a CS major and took 141,) is $(q \wedge r) \rightarrow p$; Note that $q$ and $r$ come second in the English but are on the left-hand side in logical notation. The second piece (she is a CS major or did not take 141) is $q \vee \neg r$.

Putting that all together gives $((q \wedge r) \rightarrow p) \wedge (q \vee \neg r)$. Quite a mouthful. ∎

Example. Consider the proposition:

I'm having pie, unless I'm having a milkshake.
The problem here is with "unless". Take $p=$"I'm having pie" and $q=$"I'm having a milkshake". The question is: What does "$p$ unless $q$" mean, logically?

The common interpretation is $\neg q \rightarrow p$, that is, "If I'm not having a milkshake, then I'm having pie.". You can justify this to yourself by writing down a truth table directly from the sentence and comparing it to $\neg q \rightarrow p$ (though the case where $p$ and $q$ are both false is still awkard; it's similar to the situation with $\rightarrow$). In any case, you may see "unless" from time to time, but we'll try to avoid it.

Tautologies and Contradictions

The first lecture noted that the proposition $((p \wedge q) \rightarrow r) \leftrightarrow (p \rightarrow (q \rightarrow r))$ had the interesting property that its truth table consisted of all true values. A simpler example of this phenomenon is $p \vee \neg p$; No matter if $p$ is true or false, $p \vee \neg p$ will be true.

Propositions with the property are called tautologies:

Definition 1.1. A proposition is called a tautology if its truth table is all true values.

You may have heard the word "tautology" before as in "But that is a tautology!". The suggestion is that whatever was just said is not interesting; It's just always true and not claiming anything. For example, if you claim "Raising taxes is the right thing to do, or raising taxes is the wrong thing to do." then you're unlikely to kick off an interesting debate, except maybe with a nihilist.

The opposite extreme is called a contradiction:

Definition 1.2. A proposition is called a contradiction if its truth table is all false values.

A simple example of a contradiction of $p \wedge \neg p$. This proposition is never true, as can be seen from its truth table. For more interesting examples, consider $p \rightarrow \neg p$

Contradictions are only slightly more interesting than tautologies; When someone utters a contradiction, at least you have the chance to declare victory, since you know they're wrong!

Logical Equivalences

The following notion of logical equivalence is both simple and very useful.

Definition 1.3. Two propositions $p,q$ are said to be equivalent if they have the same truth tables. We write $p \equiv q$ to indicate that $p$ and $q$ are equivalent.

The definition is simple because no matter how hairy two propositions are, you can always derive their truth tables mechanistically and then compare them. Why it should be useful will hopefully be apparent later. At a high level, when you're asked to prove a mathematical statement is true, a common trick is to translate it to a proposition $p$, manipulate it into an equivalent proposition $q$, and then prove the equivalent proposition instead. The rest of this section records a set of equivalences that are "universal" in a sense we will explain in the next section.

Some basic equivalences. You can check the following equivalences by computing truth tables:

  • $p \wedge q \equiv q \wedge p$     (Commutativity of $\wedge$)
  • $p \vee q \equiv q \vee p$     (Commutativity of $\vee$)
  • $(p \wedge q)\wedge r \equiv p \wedge (q \wedge r)$     (Associativity of $\wedge$)
  • $(p \vee q)\vee r \equiv p \vee (q \vee r)$     (Associativity of $\vee$)
  • $p \vee (q\wedge r) \equiv (p \vee q) \wedge (p \vee r)$     (Distributive law)
  • $p \wedge (q\vee r) \equiv (p \wedge q) \vee (p \wedge r)$     (Distributive law)
  • $p \leftrightarrow q \equiv (p \rightarrow q) \wedge (q \rightarrow p)$     (Biconditional equivalence)
In addition to the truth tables, you can intuit why these equivalences should hold. For example, in the last equivalence, if $p$ is true then both sides are true; If $p$ is false, then both sides are true exactly when $q$ and $r$ are both true.

De Morgan's Laws. Here are two more famous equivalences:

  • $\neg(p \wedge q) \equiv \neg p \vee \neg q$     (De Morgan's laws)
  • $\neg(p \vee q) \equiv \neg p \wedge \neg q$     (De Morgan's laws)
These equivalences are also fairly intuitive. In fact, you've probably thought through them at some point while programming. For example, the code

if( !((x>0) and (y==1)) ) {
    // do something
}

is equivalent to the code

if( (x<=0) or (y!=1) ) {
    // do something
}

This is another form of De Morgan's Laws.

Some more basic equivalences. Below let us write $T$ as a placeholder for any proposition that is a tautology, similarly $F$ for a contradiction.

  • $p\wedge T \equiv p$ and $p \vee F \equiv p$     (Identity laws)
  • $p\vee T \equiv T$ and $p \wedge F \equiv F$     (Domination laws)
  • $p\vee p \equiv p$ and $p \wedge p \equiv p$     (Idempotence of $\vee$ and $\wedge$)
  • $p\vee (p \wedge q)\equiv p$ and $p \wedge (p\vee q) \equiv p$     (Absorption laws)
  • $p\vee \neg p \equiv T$ and $p\wedge \neg p \equiv F$     (Negation laws)
  • $\neg \neg p \equiv p$     (Double negation)
  • $\neg T \equiv F$ and $\neg F \equiv T$     (Tautology or Contradiction Negation)

Most of these are so simple that you may wonder why we bother to record them. The reason is that we plan to use them in the section for showing more complicated equivalences.

Implication. Of all the equivalences in this section, this one is probably the least familiar:

  • $p \rightarrow q \equiv \neg p \vee q$     (Implication equivalence)
Let's look at the truth tables for these:
$p$ $q$ $\quad$ $p \rightarrow q$ $\neg p$ $\neg p \vee q$
$T$ $T$ $T$ $F$ $T$
$T$ $F$ $F$ $F$ $F$
$F$ $T$ $T$ $T$ $T$
$F$ $F$ $T$ $T$ $T$

The truth tables match, so they're definitely equivalent. But why? One way to understand it is that when we assert $p\rightarrow q$, if $p$ is not true, then we're done and it holds. But if $p$ is true, then we need $q$ to hold; This is exactly what the right-hand side of the equivalence says.

Proving Equivalences

In principle, any equivalence can be proven by writing out the truth tables of the given propositions and checking that they are equal. This has two drawbacks. First, if there are several atomic propositions, then the truth table will be really large (even with only four atomic propositions its annoying to do this by hand). Second, and more importantly for us, truth tables alone don't help us understand why the equivalence holds.

This section describe a different approach to establishing that two propositions are equivalent. It is based on repeatedly applying the simple observation that if $p\equiv q$ and $q \equiv r$, then $p \equiv r$. (Take a second to think about that; It's true because all three must have the same truth table.) It turns out that if any two equivalent propositions can be shown equivalent by repeated applications of the only the basic equivalences in the previous section. (In fact, only a small subset of them is needed, but having them all at our disposal will make the process much less painful.)

Equivalence of the contrapositive. Here is a particularly useful equivalence: $$\begin{aligned}p\rightarrow q \equiv \neg q \rightarrow \neg p.\end{aligned}$$ In other words, an implication is equivalent to its contrapositive! This isn't true for converses and inverses. Later we'll learn about "switching to the contrapositive" as a very useful trick in finding proofs.

Let's prove it using the equivalences from the previous section. To do this, we list a sequence of equivalences and note which one we're applying on each line. $$\begin{aligned} p \rightarrow q &\equiv \neg p \vee q & \text{(Implication equivalence)} \\ &\equiv q \vee \neg p & \text{(Commutativity of $\vee$)} \\ &\equiv \neg\neg q \vee \neg p & \text{(Double negation)} \\ &\equiv \neg q \rightarrow \neg p & \text{(Implication equivalence)} \end{aligned}$$ The first two lines are direct applications of the known equivalences. The last one is a bit tricky; We're applying the Implication equivalence in the first reverse direction (i.e., starting with $\neg s \vee t$ and turning this into $s \rightarrow t$; we did this with $s = \neg q$ and $t=\neg p$.

Example. Using the equivalence of the contrapositive, "If it is Wednesday, then I buy a milkshake" is equivalent to "If I don't buy a milkshake, then it is not Wednesday". It's worth appreciating that these are equivalent logically, but in daily life an English speaker may argue that there are unspoken assumptions in those statements that make them not equivalent. For instance, you might make the above statement even if you don't intend to buy a milkshake on Wednesday of Thanksgiving break. But in logic and mathematics in general, we interpret every statement completely at face value (sometimes to the point of irritating our colleagues!).∎

Example. Let's show that $$ (p \wedge q) \vee (\neg p \wedge q) \vee (\neg p \wedge \neg q) \equiv \neg p \vee q. $$

Actually guessing that this equivalence might hold is sort of intuitive, if you think the possibilities with $p$ is true or false and then for $q$.

In any case, here's the proof. Note that it is important that each step is labeled with the equivalence being applied.

$$\begin{aligned} (p \wedge q) \vee (\neg p \wedge q) \vee (\neg p \wedge \neg q) & \equiv (p \wedge q) \vee [(\neg p \wedge q) \vee (\neg p \wedge \neg q)] & \text{(Associativity of $\vee$)} \\ & \equiv (p \wedge q) \vee [\neg p \wedge (q \vee \neg q)] & \text{(Distribution law)} \\ & \equiv (p \wedge q) \vee [\neg p \wedge T] & \text{(Negation law)} \\ & \equiv (p \wedge q) \vee \neg p & \text{(Identity law)} \\ & \equiv \neg p \vee (p \wedge q) & \text{(Commutativity of $\vee$)} \\ & \equiv (\neg p \vee p) \wedge (\neg p \vee q) & \text{(Distribution law)} \\ & \equiv T \wedge (\neg p \vee q) & \text{(Negation law)} \\ & \equiv \neg p \vee q. & \text{(Identity law)} \\ \end{aligned} $$

Finding proofs like that just takes a little practice. One strategy is to just pattern-match and see which laws looks useful, or likely to produce a simpler proposition.∎

One might ask: What's the point of proving equivalences like this, at least in this class? It's a good question, actually. It's possible to go forward in learning math without ever doing this carefully. But at this stage, our goal is become completely comfortable with manipulating logical propositions so that we can do it almost automatically later, and these proofs are good exercises. These proofs are also our first encounter with the precision required in such proofs. You can compare this to other math classes you've likely taken, where you don't need to explicitly justify steps that use $x+y=y+x$ (the commutativity of addition). After some initial stage, this class too will return to that level of reasoning, but it's good to pause and appreciate how it is justified.
