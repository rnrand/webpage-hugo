Greatest Common Divisors and Euclid's Algorithm

This lecture will cover greatest common divisors (GCDs) and build towards one of the most famous algorithms of all time: Euclid's algorithm for computing GCDs. We begin with some definitions.

Definition 7.1. Let $d \gt 0$ be an integer. We define the functions $n \mathop{\mathbf{div}} d = q$ and $n \mathop{\mathbf{mod}} d = r$, where $q$ and $r$ are as defined by the Division Theorem (6.6).

For example, $17 \mathop{\mathbf{div}} 5= 3$ and $17 \mathop{\mathbf{mod}} 5= 2$.

These functions are defined as in the Rosen textbook and are standard definitions. We can think of $\mathbf{div}$ as the integer division operation in many programming languages that just returns the quotient and $\mathbf{mod}$ is the modulus operator.

Rigorously speaking, the domain of both functions is $\mathbb{Z}\times\mathbb{N}$ (i.e., they take $n$ and $d$ as input). Instead of writing $\mathop{\mathbf{div}}(n,d) = q$, we use the "infix" notation in the definition, but it is not any less rigorous.

Definition 7.2. The set of divisors of $n$ is defined by $\operatorname{Div}(n) = \{a : a \mid n\}$.

For example, $\operatorname{Div}(10) = \{-10,-5,-2,-1,1,2,5,10\}$. (Sorry for messing that up in Lecture!)

This is a nice definition because it is obvious what the common divisors of $m$ and $n$ are supposed to be: the intersection of $\operatorname{Div}(m)$ and $\operatorname{Div}(n)$.

Definition 7.3. If $m$ and $n$ are integers with $m \neq 0$ or $n \neq 0$, then the greatest common divisor of $m$ and $n$, denoted $\gcd(m,n)$, is the largest element of $\operatorname{Div}(m) \cap \operatorname{Div}(n)$.

From the way we've defined this, it's not entirely obvious that the GCD should exist. Without loss of generality, let $m \neq 0$ (the phrase "without loss of generality" means that our assumption does not materially change the proof that follows; We know that $m\neq 0$ or $n\neq 0$, and which case holds will only affect the notation going forward.).

Since $m\neq 0$, $\operatorname{Div}(m)$ has a largest element $|m|$ and a smallest element $-|m|$ (Exercise: Carefully verify this; You can use the fact that if $a|b$ and $b>0$, then $a\leq b$, which will be proved in the discussion sections.). This means that $\operatorname{Div}(m)$ is finite and that $\operatorname{Div}(m) \cap \operatorname{Div}(n)$ is also finite. Since 1 is a divisor for every integer, we know that $1 \in \operatorname{Div}(m) \cap \operatorname{Div}(n)$, which means that $\operatorname{Div}(m) \cap \operatorname{Div}(n)$ is non-empty. Since it's not empty and finite, the set $\operatorname{Div}(m) \cap \operatorname{Div}(n)$ must contain a largest element, which is defined to be the GCD.

Bézout's Identity

From the Division Theorem, we get the following result about the GCD by Étienne Bézout in the mid-1700s.

Theorem 7.4 (Bézout's Identity). For all integers $m$ and $n$ where $m$ or $n$ is nonzero, there exist integers $a$ and $b$ such that $\gcd(m,n) = a m + bn$.

This theorem is harder to prove than our examples from last time. It is also our first example of a proof that becomes much easier using the contrapositive.

Proof. Consider the set $S = \{am + bn \mid a,b \in \mathbb Z\}$. Then $S$ contains at least one positive element (say $mm + nn = m^2 +n^2$, which is positive because at least one of $m,n$ is non-zero). Therefore $S$ contains a smallest positive element, which we can denote $\min (S \cap \mathbb{N})$. We will show that this number is equal to $\gcd(m,n)$.

Our proof will proceed by proving the following two lemmas. We first state them and show how they imply the theorem.

Lemma 1. For all $c\in S \cap \mathbb{N}$, if $c = \min (S \cap \mathbb{N})$ then $c \in \operatorname{Div}(m) \cap \operatorname{Div}(n)$.
Lemma 2. For all $c \in \operatorname{Div}(m) \cap \operatorname{Div}(n)$, $c \leq \min (S \cap \mathbb{N})$.
Now let's assume these lemmas are true and prove the theorem. By the first lemma, $\min (S \cap \mathbb{N})$ is a common divisor of $m$ and $n$. By the second lemma, any other common divisor is less than or equal to $\min (S \cap \mathbb{N})$. Thus $\min (S \cap \mathbb{N})$ is, by definition, equal to $\gcd(m,n)$. $$\tag*{$\Box$}$$ We declared victory using the $\Box$ symbol, but we still have to prove the lemmas.

Proof of Lemma 1. We employ a new trick here: Let's prove the contrapositive, which is equivalent to the lemma. This means we only need to prove:

Contrapositive of Lemma 1. For all $c\in S \cap \mathbb{N}$, if $c \notin \operatorname{Div}(m) \cap \operatorname{Div}(n)$, then $c \neq \min (S \cap \mathbb{N})$.
It's a little hard to explain why this is easier to prove, but it is a common phenomenon. Let's dive in: Let $c\in S \cap \mathbb{N}$, and assume $c \notin \operatorname{Div}(m) \cap \operatorname{Div}(n)$. Without loss of generality, assume $c$ does not divide $m$. We must show that $c \neq \min (S \cap \mathbb{N})$. We'll do this by finding a smaller element of that set.

Since $c\in S$, there exist $a,b\in\mathbb{Z}$ such that $c=am+bn$. By the Division Theorem, there exist $q,r\in\mathbb{Z}$ such that $m = qc +r$ and $0\leq r \lt c$. We know that $r\neq 0$, because otherwise $c$ would divide $m$. We also know that $r\in S \cap \mathbb{N}$, since we can write \begin{align*} r &= m - qc \\ &= m - q(am + bn) \\ &= (1-qa)m + (-bq)n \in S. \end{align*} But $r \in S \cap \mathbb{N}$ and $r\lt c$, so $c \neq \min (S \cap \mathbb{N})$, as desired. $$\tag*{$\Box$}$$ Next up is the proof of the second lemma:

Proof of Lemma 2. We can do this one directly. Let $c\in \operatorname{Div}(m) \cap \operatorname{Div}(n)$. We will show that $c$ divides $\min (S \cap \mathbb{N})$. Since $\min (S \cap \mathbb{N})$ is positive, this will show that $c \leq \min (S \cap \mathbb{N})$, as desired.

Our assumption on $c$ implies there exist $s,t\in\mathbb{Z}$ such that $cs=m$ and $ct=n$. Since $\min (S \cap \mathbb{N})\in S$, there exist there exist $a,b\in\mathbb{Z}$ such that $\min (S \cap \mathbb{N})=am+bn$. Then we have \begin{align*} \min (S \cap \mathbb{N}) &= am + bn \\ &= acs + bct \\ &= c \cdot (as + bt) \end{align*} Therefore, $c \mid \min (S \cap \mathbb{N})$, completing the proof. $$\tag*{$\Box$}$$

Euclid's Algorithm

Bézout's identity is interesting in that it provides a characterization for the GCD of $m$ and $n$. Suppose we have $m$, $n$, and what we think is $\gcd(m,n)$. Is there a way to check that the GCD is really correct without computing it? If we had the coefficients of Bézout's identity for $m$ and $n$, then we could check very easily. Of course, we're getting a bit ahead of ourselves, but keep this idea in mind while we tackle a more immediate question: how does one compute the GCD?

The obvious way to do this is to compute the set of divisors for $m$ and $n$, compute their intersection, and take the largest element in the intersection. The big problem with this approach is that factoring integers is a notoriously hard problem to solve efficiently. There are some heuristics that we can apply and some tricks that we can pull, but in general we do not have a fast algorithm for factoring numbers. This fact happens to be the backbone of many public-key cryptographic systems we use today.

However, if all we care about is the greatest common divisor and not any of the other divisors, then the following theorem will get us there efficiently.

Theorem 7.5. For all positive integers $a,b$, $$ \gcd(a,b) = \gcd(b,a \mathop{\mathbf{mod}} b). $$ This theorem is actually true for negative integers as well, but we'll settle for this version.

Proof. Let $a$ and $b$ be arbitrary positive integers. Let $q$ be such that $a = qb + r$. (Note that we're using that $b$ is positive and thus non-zero in order to apply the Division Theorem here.) Let $d = \gcd(a,b)$ and $r=a \mathop{\mathbf{mod}} b$. We will show that $d = \gcd(b,r)$ as well.

We first show that $d$ is a common divisor of $b$ and $r$; For this we only need to show that $d|r$. Since $d \mid q$, $d\mid qb$. We also know that $d|a$, so putting these together we have that $d\mid (a-qb)$, i.e. $d|r$. Thus $d$ is a common divisor of $b$ and $r$.

Now consider an arbitrary common divisor $c$ of $b$ and $r$. If we can show $c\leq d$, then we're done; We will have shown that $d$ is larger than any other common divisor of $a$ and $b$.

Since $c \mid b$ and $c \mid r$, we have that $c \mid (qb+r) = a$, so $c$ is also a common divisor for $a$. Thus $c$ is a common divisor of $a$ and $b$, and hence it must be less than their greatest common divisor, which is $d$. Therefore, $\gcd(b,r) = d = \gcd(a,b)$. $$\tag*{$\Box$}$$

This result gives us a way to compute the GCD: $$\gcd(a,b) = \begin{cases} a & \text{if $b = 0$,} \\ \gcd(b, a \mathop{\mathbf{mod}} b) & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}$$

This algorithm is called Euclid's algorithm, named after Euclid who describes it in the Elements. Euclid is the Greek mathematician from around 300 BC who, in the Elements, describes a lot of the elementary geometry, algebra, and number theory that we learn in school.

Example 5.4. Suppose we want to compute the GCD of 232 and 72. We apply Euclid's algorithm as follows: \begin{align*} \gcd(232,72) &= \gcd(72,16) \\ &= \gcd(16,8) \\ &= \gcd(8,0) \\ &= 8 \end{align*}

This leads us to the notion of the existence of an Extended Euclidean algorithm. The EEA is an algorithm that computes the GCD of $m$ and $n$ and the coefficients $a$ and $b$ from Bézout's lemma. We can treat these coefficients as a certificate or sanity test that the GCD we computed was really correct without having recompute the entire thing later on. The EEA gives us a way to compute the GCD so that without doing a lot of extra work, $a$ and $b$ fall out almost for free.

Here are some well-known exercise for practice finding proofs. They are significantly more challenging that the previous exercises.

  • An integer $a$ is even if $2\mid a$. Prove that if $a$ is even, then $\gcd(a,a+2)=2$.
  • Let $a$ be a positive integer. Find $\gcd(a,a+1)$, and prove your answer is correct.
  • Let $a,b,c$ be a positive integer. Find $\gcd(ac,bc)$ in terms of $\gcd(a,b)$, and prove your answer is correct.
  • Prove that for all $a,b,c\in \mathbb{Z}$ with $b\neq 0$, there exist $x,y\in\mathbb{Z}$ if and only if $\gcd(a,b)|c$. (Your proof will use Bézout's Identity. Remember to prove "both directions" of the if-and-only-if.)
  • Prove that for any $a,b\in\mathbb{N}$, if $\gcd(a,b)=1$ then $\gcd(a,b^2)=1$.
  • Prove that for any $a,b\in\mathbb{N}$, if $\gcd(a,b)=1$ then $\gcd(a^2,b^2)=1$. (You can use the previous exercise to give short proof.)
