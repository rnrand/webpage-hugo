Discrete Probability Spaces

This lecture initiates the development of probability theory that is general enough to model cases where outcomes might have different probabilities. Our first task will be to define what is a valid way of assigning probabilities. Obviously we want to rule out things like assigning negative probabilities, or probabilities that do not add up to $1$. The next definition gives a definition of consistency for some assignment of numbers to represent probabilities of events. This version of the definition is an interesting example of mathematicians purifying necessary rules down into a short, elegant list that happens to imply everything we'd expect.

In this definition, we use $2^\Omega$ to mean the power set of $\Omega$ (instead of $P(\Omega)$, which may be confusing in this context).

Definition 18.1. A discrete probability space is a pair $(\Omega,\Pr)$ where $\Omega$ is a set (called the sample space) and $\Pr : 2^\Omega \to \mathbb R$ is a function satisfying the following three requirements (called "axioms of probability"):

  1. (1st axiom) For all $E \subseteq \Omega, \Pr(E) \geq 0$.
  2. (2nd axiom) $\Pr(\Omega) = 1$.
  3. (3rd axiom) For all sequences of disjoint events $E_1,E_2,\ldots$ (where $E_i \subseteq \Omega$ for all $i$), $$ \Pr\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty E_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty \Pr(E_i). $$

The motivation for this definition may seem obscure; it came out of nowhere. As we said above, it is the agreed-upon definition because it implies everything that we will eventually expect to be true. But let's take it at face value and understand the axioms. The first says that no probabilities are negative, which is intuitively a reasonable requirement. The second says that the probability assigned to $\Omega$, the entire space, is $1$. This makes sense because $\Omega$ contains every outcome that we are modeling (as in naive probability).

The third condition is more complicated. Don't let the infinities intimidate you. The infinite union is simply $$ \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty E_i = \{ x \ | \ \exists i, x\in E_i\}, $$ which is no different from finite unions. The sum in this rule is, sadly, a limit which needs to be defined with calculus. We will avoid dealing with these sums too much, and when it comes to proofs we will appeal to calculus without proving things about sums ourselves.

In any case, that third rule says that when you want the probability of a union of disjoint events, you can just add up their individual probabilities.

You may have noticed that we call this a discrete probability space. There is indeed a more general notion of probability space that is able to handle "continuous" spaces like choosing real numbers. We won't discuss this direction further because it gets highly technical (with the sort of technicalities we do not want to study now).

The magic of the definition is that you can prove lots of other stuff assuming only it. Here are some examples.

Theorem 18.2. Let $(\Omega,\Pr)$ be a discrete probability space. Then $\Pr(\emptyset)=0$.

Proof. Define an infinite sequence $E_1,E_2,\ldots$ by $E_i = \emptyset$ for all $i$. Then these events are disjoint, and $\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty E_i = \emptyset$. By the third axiom of probability, $$ \Pr(\emptyset) = \Pr\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty E_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty \Pr(E_i) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty \Pr(\emptyset). $$ If $\Pr(\emptyset)\neq 0$ then the right hand side would diverge while the left hand side would not, a contradiction. $\tag*{$\Box$}$

Let's prove some more!

Theorem 18.3. Let $(\Omega,\Pr)$ be a discrete probability space. Then for any finite sequence of disjoint events $E_1,\ldots,E_n$, $$ \Pr\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n E_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^n \Pr(E_i). $$

Proof. This may feel trivial, but it's not: We only now it's true for infinite sequences. So consider a finite sequence $E_1,\ldots,E_n$. Define $E_i = \emptyset$ for all $i>n$, and apply the third axiom to this sequence (which is still disjoint): \begin{align*} \Pr\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n E_i\right) &= \Pr\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty E_i\right) \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^\infty \Pr(E_i) & \text{(by the third axiom)} \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n \Pr(E_i) + \sum_{i=n+1}^\infty \Pr(E_i)\\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n \Pr(E_i). & \text{(by Theorem 18.2)} \end{align*} $\tag*{$\Box$}$

A simple form of Theorem 18.3 is when $n=2$: It says that for disjoint events $E,F$, $\Pr(E\cup F) = \Pr(E) + \Pr(F)$.

Corollary 18.4. Let $(\Omega,\Pr)$ be a discrete probability space and $E\subseteq \Omega$ be an event. Then $\Pr(E^c) = 1 - \Pr(E)$.

Proof. The events $E$ and $E^c$ are disjoint and their union is $\Omega$. By the second axiom and Theorem 18.3, $1 = \Pr(\Omega) = \Pr(E\cup E^c) = \Pr(E) + \Pr(E^c)$. $\tag*{$\Box$}$

The next theorem gets a formula for the case when the events are not disjoint.

Theorem 18.5. Let $(\Omega,\Pr)$ be a discrete probability space. Then for all events $E,F$, $$ \Pr(E\cup F) = \Pr(E) + \Pr(F) - \Pr(E \cap F). $$

Proof. This proof takes a bit more work. Our general strategy with proofs like this is always to try and "disjointify": Barring using a previous theorem, our only real tool is the third axiom, which only applies to disjoint event. So let's start by writing $E\cup F = E \cup (F\cap E^c)$, which you can justify by direct proof or by drawing a Venn diagram. We have that $E$ and $F\cap E^c$ are disjoint, so by Theorem 18.3 $$ \Pr(E\cup F) = \Pr(E) + \Pr(F\cap E^c). $$

We can complete the proof by showing that $\Pr(F\cap E^c) = \Pr(F)-\Pr(E\cap F)$. But as soon as we write that, the strategy presents itself: We have $F= (F \cap E) \cup (F\cap E^c)$, and this is a union of disjoint sets, so $$ \Pr(F)= \Pr(F \cap E) + \Pr(F\cap E^c) $$ by Theorem 18.3. Plugging this in gives the theorem. $\tag*{$\Box$}$

Inclusion-Exclusion

Theorem 18.5 is a special case of something called inclusion-exclusion. It is fairly intuitive if you draw a Venn diagram and think of the formula as correcting for overcounting: It adds up two circles, and then subtracts the intersection, which gets counted twice.

For three sets $E,F,G$, Theorem 18.5 generalizes to this formula: \begin{align*} \Pr(E\cup F\cup G) = & \Pr(E) + \Pr(F) +\Pr(G) \\ & - \Pr(E \cap F) - \Pr(E \cap G) - \Pr(F \cap G) \\ & + \Pr(E\cap F\cap G). \end{align*}

Once again, drawing a Venn diagram (or looking to the diagrams in the Rosen or Blitzstein-Hwang texts) is helpful in understanding this formula. One is add up the "sizes" of the three sets, but this counts their intersections twice and their three-wise intersection three times. So we substract their pairwise intersections. But now we've gone too far with the three-wise intersection: We counted it three time and subtracted it three times, so we add it back.

Here is the general theorem, which can be proved by induction (we'll use this but we won't prove it; Like life, 9 week quarters are short!).

Theorem 18.6. Let $(\Omega,\Pr)$ be a discrete probability space. Then for all events $E_1,E_2,\ldots,E_n$, \begin{align*} \Pr\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n E_i\right) = \sum_{1 \leq i \leq n} \Pr(E_i) - \sum_{1 \leq i\lt j \leq n} \Pr(E_i\cap E_j) & + \sum_{1 \leq i\lt j \lt k \leq n} \Pr(E_i\cap E_j\cap E_k) - \cdots \\ & + (-1)^{n+1}\Pr(E_1 \cap \cdots \cap E_n). \end{align*}

The notation in the theorem means that the first sum is over all of the individual events, the second sum is over all $\binom{n}{2}$ pairs of events, and in general the $i$-th sum is over all $\binom{n}{i}$ sets of $i$ events.

Inclusion-exclusion is usually a last resort for probability and counting problems, and it can get hairy even for moderately sized $n$. It can however give nice formulas when the problem has a lot symmetry, like the next two examples.

Example. We say that poker hand is void in a suit if contains no cards of that suit. What is the probability that a $10$-card poker hand is void in at least one suit?

You can try this by counting the complement, but it looks pretty complicated. Let's use inclusion-exclusion. Define events $E_1,E_2,E_3,E_4$ that correspond to being void in each of the four suits. We want $\Pr(E_1 \cup E_2 \cup E_3 \cup E_4)$.

We first calculate $\Pr(E_i)=\binom{39}{10}/\binom{52}{10}$, because being void in a suit means avoiding the 13 cards of the suit, i.e. picking from the remaining 39 cards. Similarly, for any $i\neq j$, $\Pr(E_i\cap E_j)=\binom{26}{10}/\binom{52}{10}$ since being void in two suits means avoiding the 26 cards of those suits. Next, for any distinct $i,j,k$, $\Pr(E_i\cap E_j\cap E_k)=\binom{13}{10}/\binom{52}{10}$ by the same reasoning. Finally, $\Pr(E_1\cap E_2\cap E_3\cap E_4) =0$; It's impossible to be void in all suits.

Now we apply Theorem 18.6. What makes this feasible is symmetry: All of the terms in the first sum are the same, and so on. Thus we get that $$ \Pr(E_1 \cup E_2 \cup E_3 \cup E_4) = \binom{4}{1}\cdot \frac{\binom{39}{10}}{\binom{52}{10}} - \binom{4}{2}\cdot \frac{\binom{26}{10}}{\binom{52}{10}} + \binom{4}{3}\cdot \frac{\binom{13}{10}}{\binom{52}{10}} - \binom{4}{4}\cdot 0. $$

Example. In class we will do de Montmort's matching problem, which you can read about in Blitzstein-Hwang; It is Example 1.6.4 on page 25.
